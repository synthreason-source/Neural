Special Containment Procedures
CONTAINMENT

SCP-7741 requires no physical containment structure. All confirmed instances of SCP-7741-A (affected personnel) are to be identified during Foundation onboarding neurological screening and flagged in personnel records under code ██████████.

SCP-7741-A individuals are prohibited from serving in roles requiring retention of screen-displayed classified material. Standard screen-based security protocols are insufficient for SCP-7741-A subjects, as they cannot be held accountable for information they are neurologically incapable of retaining.

Accommodation adjustments — including printed hard-copy briefings, verbal briefings with audio records, and printed visual aids — are to be implemented for all SCP-7741-A personnel per ████ Directive 44-C.

Under no circumstances should SCP-7741-A personnel be assigned to cognitohazard review via screen-based media. Inability to retain viewed content creates an unquantifiable exposure risk. See Incident Report ██████ for precedent.

Object Description

SCP-7741 is a neurological phenomenon manifesting as a total and permanent inability to form, retain, or retrieve visual memories of any digital display device — including computer monitors, tablet screens, televisions, and LED panels of all refresh rates and luminance profiles.

Affected individuals (hereafter SCP-7741-A) demonstrate complete and normal perceptual processing of screen content in real time. They can read text, navigate interfaces, respond to visual stimuli, and describe images accurately while looking at them. Yet within seconds of removing their gaze, all visual content vanishes completely from accessible memory — leaving no trace in either short-term or long-term storage.

The anomaly is absolute. Repeated exposures do not strengthen retention. No pharmacological or cognitive intervention has produced measurable improvement. The condition is not progressive — it does not extend to other visual domains and does not worsen over time.

"I know I was looking at something. I can feel the light. The brightness. The shape of it all. But the image itself — it is simply not there."

— SCP-7741-A-03, subject interview, debrief session 7

SCP-7741-A subjects often develop compensatory strategies: photographing screens, reading aloud for audio recall, or copying text by hand. These workarounds preserve the content of what was displayed but not the visual experience — subjects cannot recall fonts, colors, layouts, or visual hierarchy from any screen-based source.

Neurological Annotations
// Annotation — Dr. ██████, Cognitive Division //

SCP-7741 most closely resembles display-specific anterograde amnesia — a selective failure of memory consolidation targeting digital visual stimuli only. Unlike classic anterograde amnesia (as seen post-hippocampal lesion in patient H.M., 1953), SCP-7741 spares all other visual memory domains entirely.

The perirhinal cortex, entorhinal cortex, and parahippocampal cortex — key nodes in the visual-to-memory encoding pathway — appear structurally and functionally intact on both structural MRI and task-based fMRI. The deficit likely originates at the level of attentional gating in the pulvinar nucleus of the thalamus, which filters visual salience signals before they reach hippocampal consolidation. It is hypothesised that the pulvinar treats screen-emitted stimuli as low-salience "noise" and systematically suppresses their transfer into long-term storage.

Alternatively — and more disturbingly — the hippocampus may be writing screen memories normally, only for them to be selectively erased during sleep-phase consolidation by an as-yet unidentified mechanism. We cannot distinguish between these hypotheses with current neuroimaging resolution.

// Annotation — Differential Diagnosis Summary //

1. Visual Agnosia (Ventral Stream Lesion): Damage to occipito-temporal regions (fusiform gyrus, V4) impairs object recognition, but subjects still form memories of having seen something. SCP-7741-A form no such trace whatsoever.

2. Aphantasia: Congenital absence of voluntary visual imagery (est. 2–3% of population). Subjects cannot mentally "picture" recalled objects but retain factual knowledge of having seen them. SCP-7741 is categorically more severe — no factual visual trace is encoded at all.

3. Category-Specific Amnesia: Documented in TBI and herpes encephalitis — where damage eliminates memory for one semantic category (e.g. living things) while sparing others. SCP-7741 mimics this architecture but targets a perceptual medium rather than a semantic category. This distinction is significant and unprecedented in the literature.

4. Functional/Psychogenic Amnesia: Psychogenic amnesia can be content-selective, typically following traumatic themes. Psychogenic origin for SCP-7741 cannot be entirely ruled out, though no trauma correlate has been identified across the 14 confirmed SCP-7741-A cases to date.

5. Digital Amnesia (Sparrow Effect): The documented tendency of non-anomalous individuals to encode less when they expect digital retrieval. SCP-7741 is categorically distinct — it functions even when subjects are explicitly instructed to memorise, are offered incentives, or are unaware the test is occurring.

// Annotation — Dr. ████, Site-19 Cognitive Unit // Controlled Testing Results //

We administered the Rey-Osterrieth Complex Figure Test to four SCP-7741-A subjects using (a) standard paper stimulus cards, and (b) identical images displayed on a calibrated monitor. Recall was tested at 3 minutes and 30 minutes post-exposure.

Paper condition: Mean recall score 94th percentile. All subjects reproduced figures with high accuracy at both intervals.

Monitor condition: Mean recall score below 1st percentile. Effectively floor-level. No subject could reproduce any element of the figure at 3-minute recall. At 30 minutes, subjects were unable to confirm they had seen any image at all.

This dissociation confirms that the deficit is stimulus-medium-specific, not content-specific. The image enters the brain. It is processed. It is described in the moment. And then — it is simply gone.

Proposed Mechanism

Working hypothesis: SCP-7741 reflects a disruption in gamma-band synchrony (30–80 Hz) between the primary visual cortex (V1–V5) and the hippocampal memory encoding system during perception of blue-spectrum photon emission from LCD/OLED backlighting. The brain processes the image — fully, accurately, in real time — but the encoding transfer signal never fires.

Memories are written to a short-term sensory buffer and then immediately overwritten, likely within the same 500ms window that normally corresponds to memory consolidation onset. The result is a system that functions like a camera with no storage medium — every frame captured in perfect fidelity, every frame immediately discarded.

It remains unresolved whether SCP-7741 is a congenital anomaly, an acquired condition, or an externally induced effect. Current evidence suggests congenital onset, but three of fourteen identified SCP-7741-A subjects report a specific onset date following ████████████████████████████████. Investigation ongoing.

Addendum 7741-Ω — Researcher Note
// Personal Note — Dr. ██████████ — NOT FOR OFFICIAL RECORD //

I have been researching SCP-7741 for ███ months. I have read every neurological scan, every interview transcript, every incident report.

Last week, I sat with SCP-7741-A-07 in the observation room. We talked for an hour. I showed him photographs — printed ones — of his family, his home, places he had been. He remembered everything. His recall was vivid and emotional.

Then I handed him a tablet with the same photographs displayed on screen. He looked at each one carefully. He smiled at the right moments. He said all the right things.

I took the tablet away.

He could not tell me what he had just seen. Not a single image. He knew he had been looking at something. He could feel the warmth of the screen on his face, the brightness.

I do not believe this is a neurological anomaly. I believe something is taking it.

This note has been reviewed and my assessment is flagged as █████████████████████. I am advised to confine my conclusions to the observable data.

— ████
